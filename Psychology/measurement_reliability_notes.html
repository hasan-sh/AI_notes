Measurement, reliability and descriptive research

Measurement scales


Research can be measured by providing two definitions; of the conceptual and operational variables.

The levels of operational variables can be coded using different scales(measurement scales):

> 3 types of quantitative variables: - categorical(or Nominal) scale: consists of names or numbers - Ordinal scale: ranked order(e.g. order of best movies); where only the orders matter - interval scale: on an equal interval(e.g. distance or weight); cannot be zero - Ratio scale: can be zero(e.g. scores in exams or numbers of ratings) 

> measurement scales are important for:
- determining the possible statistical operations
- determining the possible conclusions

  • categorical(or Nominal) variables: coded with meaningful names or not meaningful numbers; numbers don't reflect what the meaning of the subjects is. (e.g. sex)
  • quantitative variables: coded with meaningful numbers. (e.g. height or weight)
  • Conceptual variable: is the subject of the research; on a theoretical level.
  • Operational variable: is the means with which different techniques can be proposed to approach the conceptual variable
    • self-report: in which participants rate/choose what suits them the best
    • operational report:
    • psychological report:
> The levels of operational variables can be coded using different scales


Reliability


Before choosing which measures to use in a study, researchers examine data, collected by them or others, to decide whether it's reliable or not.

> Establishing the reliability of a measure is an empirical question.

> the variability in the process u obtain from the reliability assessment gives an indication of whether the measurement are reliable or not.

  • observed score = true score + error + score
  • true score: the real value
  • error score: the difference between the measured value and the real value which is affected by
    • the test(situation)
    • the person

> If an operationalization is reliable, it will yield a consistent pattern of scores every time.

Assessments(types) of reliability


> depending on how a variable was operationalized. - test-retest reliability: to get consistent scores over time(i.e. doing a measure by a researcher, yields consistent results for hem or her) 

>  information of the extent to which the test is capable of

  • interrater reliability: consistent scores must be obtained independency of who measured the variable
> is most relevant for observational measures

  • internal reliability: consistent pattern of answers must be obtained by a participant, no matter who the researcher's phrased the question; this applies only to self-report scales
> e.g. Diener's five-item subjective well-being scale; participants should always disagree or agree on all 5 questions; then we can say the scale has internal reliability

  • experimental reliability: in replicating research that has been done before, the reliability rate would be higher if the result will be equal or very similar

There are two ways to analyse data and see whether it's reliable.

> Reliability quantification

  • Scatterplot: a good picture of a plane where u show the agreement of the data, based on test-retest or interrater reliability
  • Correlation coefficient r: provides the direction and the strength of the association between the two variables; no matter what scale was used.

Descriptive research


  • Observational research: in which u look at behavior; people animals or events. ANd you record whats happening
    • to describe nature; e.g. evolution theory
    • to generate hypotheses(not to test hypotheses!; for this you should do a control experiment)
      • e.g. bystander effect; the more ppl there are, the more likely those ppl will help ppl in emergency situations.
> this hypotheses isn't true in all cases, there are many factors that play in and it differs from one person to another.

Problems:

  • people are not always good at observation
  • low internal validity; hard to obtain any casual conclusions
  • threats construct validity; do you measure what you measure!
    • Observer bias; ppl could have make assumptions based on their experiences
    • Observer effects; result might come in prefer of the expectation of the observer
> e.g. horse was trained to do somemath, but he awas responding to the trainer

  • Reactivity: ppl change behavior when they know they're been observed
    • unobtrusive observations
    • wait it out strategy(participant observation)
    • unobtrusive measures
